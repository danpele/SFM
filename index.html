Chapter 1: Data Sources & Returns

Question 1: Simple Return

What is the formula for simple (net) return $R_t$?

  • A) $\ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$
  • B) $P_t - P_{t-1}$
  • C) $(P_t - P_{t-1}) / P_{t-1}$
  • D) $P_t / P_{t-1}$
Correct! The simple return is $R_t = (P_t - P_{t-1}) / P_{t-1}$, measuring the percentage change in price.
Incorrect. The correct answer is C. $R_t = (P_t - P_{t-1}) / P_{t-1}$.

Question 2: Log Return

What is the formula for log (continuously compounded) return $r_t$?

  • A) $(P_t - P_{t-1}) / P_{t-1}$
  • B) $\ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$
  • C) $P_t \cdot P_{t-1}$
  • D) $\exp(P_t) - 1$
Correct! The log return is $r_t = \ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$, also known as the continuously compounded return.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. $r_t = \ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$.

Question 3: Variance Drag

What is "variance drag" in the context of returns?

  • A) Variance increases linearly over time
  • B) The arithmetic mean return exceeds the geometric mean by approximately $\sigma^2/2$
  • C) Returns with high variance always have negative mean
  • D) A method for reducing portfolio variance
Correct! Variance drag means the geometric mean is lower than the arithmetic mean by approximately $\sigma^2/2$, eroding compounded wealth.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Variance drag is the gap between arithmetic and geometric mean returns ($\approx \sigma^2/2$).

Question 4: Log Returns Advantage

Why are log returns preferred for statistical modeling?

  • A) They are always positive
  • B) They are additive over time and approximately normally distributed
  • C) They are easier to compute
  • D) They never have outliers
Correct! Log returns are time-additive ($r_{1:T} = \sum r_t$) and better approximated by the normal distribution than simple returns.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Log returns are additive over time and closer to normality.

Question 5: ADF Test

What does the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test assess?

  • A) Normality of returns
  • B) Presence of a unit root (non-stationarity)
  • C) Heteroscedasticity in residuals
  • D) Autocorrelation at all lags
Correct! The ADF test has $H_0$: unit root (non-stationary) vs $H_1$: stationary.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The ADF test checks for a unit root, indicating non-stationarity.

Question 6: Jarque-Bera Test

What does the Jarque-Bera test evaluate?

  • A) Stationarity of the time series
  • B) Whether data follows a normal distribution (via skewness and kurtosis)
  • C) Serial correlation in residuals
  • D) Presence of unit roots
Correct! The Jarque-Bera test checks normality by testing whether skewness $= 0$ and excess kurtosis $= 0$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Jarque-Bera tests normality using skewness and kurtosis.

Question 7: Parkinson Estimator

Which volatility estimator uses the high-low price range?

  • A) Sample standard deviation
  • B) Parkinson estimator
  • C) EWMA
  • D) Realized volatility
Correct! The Parkinson (1980) estimator uses the daily high-low range: $\sigma^2_P = \frac{1}{4n\ln 2}\sum (\ln H_i - \ln L_i)^2$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The Parkinson estimator uses high-low price ranges.

Question 8: Sharpe Ratio

What does a Sharpe ratio measure?

  • A) Total return of an asset
  • B) Excess return per unit of risk (standard deviation)
  • C) The correlation between two assets
  • D) Maximum drawdown of a portfolio
Correct! The Sharpe ratio is $S = (\bar{r} - r_f) / \sigma$, measuring risk-adjusted return.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The Sharpe ratio measures excess return per unit of risk.

Question 9: OHLC Data

What does OHLC data represent?

  • A) Only closing prices
  • B) Open, High, Low, Close prices for each period
  • C) Overnight returns only
  • D) Order flow and trade data
Correct! OHLC stands for Open, High, Low, Close — the four key prices recorded each trading period.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. OHLC = Open, High, Low, Close prices per period.

Question 10: Leverage Effect

What is the "leverage effect" in financial markets?

  • A) Using borrowed money to invest
  • B) Negative returns tend to increase future volatility more than positive returns of the same magnitude
  • C) Large firms have less volatile stock prices
  • D) Interest rates directly determine stock prices
Correct! The leverage effect describes the asymmetric response of volatility to negative vs. positive returns.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The leverage effect means negative returns increase volatility more than positive returns.

Chapter 2: Statistical Distributions in Capital Markets

Question 1: Motivation

Why are stable distributions needed to model financial returns?

  • A) Financial returns are always normally distributed
  • B) Financial returns exhibit heavy tails and skewness that the normal distribution cannot capture
  • C) The normal distribution overestimates extreme events
  • D) Stable distributions have simpler formulas than the normal
Correct! The Gaussian assumption underestimates the probability of extreme events (crashes, booms). Stable distributions allow for heavy tails and asymmetry.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Financial returns exhibit heavy tails and skewness that the normal distribution cannot capture.

Question 2: Generalized CLT

What does the Generalized Central Limit Theorem (GCLT) state?

  • A) All sums of random variables converge to the normal distribution
  • B) Normalized sums of heavy-tailed i.i.d. variables converge to a stable distribution
  • C) The CLT only works for financial data
  • D) Heavy-tailed distributions have no limit theorems
Correct! The GCLT generalizes the classical CLT: when the finite variance assumption is relaxed, normalized sums converge to stable laws with $\alpha < 2$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The GCLT shows sums of heavy-tailed variables converge to stable distributions.

Question 3: Historical Contribution

Who first applied stable distributions to model speculative prices?

  • A) Paul Lévy (1925)
  • B) Benoît Mandelbrot (1963)
  • C) John P. Nolan (2003)
  • D) Eugene Fama (1970)
Correct! Mandelbrot (1963) first applied stable distributions to model cotton prices, showing financial data deviate significantly from normality. Fama (1965) extended this to stock returns.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Mandelbrot (1963) pioneered the use of stable distributions in finance.

Question 4: Four Parameters

A stable distribution $X \sim S(\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta)$ is characterized by four parameters. What does $\beta \in [-1, 1]$ control?

  • A) Tail heaviness
  • B) Skewness (asymmetry)
  • C) Scale (spread)
  • D) Location (shift)
Correct! $\beta$ controls asymmetry: $\beta = 0$ is symmetric, $\beta > 0$ means heavier right tail, $\beta < 0$ means heavier left tail.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. $\beta$ controls skewness. ($\alpha$ controls tail heaviness, $\gamma$ controls scale, $\delta$ controls location.)

Question 5: Special Cases

Which three stable distributions have closed-form probability density functions?

  • A) Normal, Exponential, Poisson
  • B) Gaussian ($\alpha=2$), Cauchy ($\alpha=1, \beta=0$), and Lévy ($\alpha=0.5, \beta=1$)
  • C) Student-$t$, Chi-squared, F-distribution
  • D) All stable distributions have closed-form PDFs
Correct! Only three stable distributions have closed-form PDFs: Gaussian, Cauchy, and Lévy. All others require numerical computation via inverse Fourier transform.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The Gaussian, Cauchy, and Lévy distributions are the only stable distributions with closed-form densities.

Question 6: VaR Comparison

For S&P 500 returns at the 0.1% level, how does stable VaR compare to Gaussian VaR?

  • A) They are approximately equal
  • B) Stable VaR is approximately 2.5 times larger than Gaussian VaR
  • C) Gaussian VaR is larger
  • D) VaR cannot be computed for stable distributions
Correct! At the 0.1% level, stable VaR ($-9.0\%$) is approximately 2.5 times larger than Gaussian VaR ($-3.6\%$), showing the normal distribution severely underestimates tail risk.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Stable VaR is ~2.5× larger, reflecting the heavier tails.

Question 7: Moments

For a stable distribution with $\alpha < 2$, which statement about moments is correct?

  • A) All moments exist
  • B) The variance is infinite; only moments of order $p < \alpha$ are finite
  • C) The mean never exists
  • D) The variance is finite but kurtosis is infinite
Correct! $E[|X|^p] < \infty$ for $0 < p < \alpha$ and $E[|X|^p] = \infty$ for $p \geq \alpha$. The mean exists only if $\alpha > 1$, and the variance exists only if $\alpha = 2$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. For $\alpha < 2$, the variance is infinite; only fractional moments of order $< \alpha$ exist.

Question 8: Estimation Methods

What estimation method did McCulloch (1986) propose for stable distribution parameters?

  • A) Maximum likelihood via gradient descent
  • B) Bayesian MCMC sampling
  • C) Quantile-based estimation using sample quantile ratios
  • D) Method of moments using variance and kurtosis
Correct! McCulloch's method uses relationships between quantile ratios and stable parameters to provide fast and simple initial estimates of $\alpha$ and $\beta$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is C. McCulloch (1986) proposed quantile-based estimation. (Method of moments fails because the variance is infinite for $\alpha < 2$.)

Question 9: Simulation

What algorithm is used to simulate stable random variables?

  • A) Box-Muller transform
  • B) Chambers-Mallows-Stuck (CMS) algorithm
  • C) Inverse CDF method
  • D) Rejection sampling from a normal distribution
Correct! The CMS (1976) algorithm generates standard stable r.v.s using a Uniform and an Exponential random variable. It is available in Python via scipy.stats.levy_stable.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. The Chambers-Mallows-Stuck (CMS) algorithm is the standard method for simulating stable random variables.

Question 10: Tail Behavior

How do stable distribution tails ($\alpha < 2$) decay compared to the Gaussian?

  • A) Exponentially, like the Gaussian
  • B) As a power law: $P(X > x) \sim C \cdot x^{-\alpha}$
  • C) They have bounded support (no tails)
  • D) Logarithmically
Correct! Stable tails follow a power law (polynomial decay), not exponential. The smaller $\alpha$, the heavier the tails. Financial returns typically have $\alpha \in [1.5, 1.9]$.
Incorrect. The correct answer is B. Stable distributions have power-law tails: $P(X > x) \sim C \cdot x^{-\alpha}$.
